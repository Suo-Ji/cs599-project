1 Introduction

Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) revisits the problem of predictive uncertainty by
placing a prior distribution over the parameters of a categorical or regression
output layer. In contrast to Bayesian Neural Networks (BNN), which require
expensive posterior inference, EDL formulates uncertainty estimation as the
optimization of a single forward pass with a Dirichlet or Normal-Inverse-Gamma
prior. The central claim is that aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty can be
separated without Monte Carlo sampling. This work evaluates EDL on a regression
benchmark with neural network backbones ranging from a Multilayer Perceptron
(MLP) to a Graph Neural Network (GNN).

Predictive calibration is reported through Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) and
interval-based metrics, specifically the Prediction Interval Coverage
Probability (PICP) and the Mean Prediction Interval Width (MPIW). The Root Mean
Squared Error (RMSE) serves as the point-estimate accuracy baseline. Across all
backbones, the goal is to determine whether EDL reduces NLL while preserving
RMSE and maintaining tight, well-calibrated prediction intervals.

2 Related Work

Prior uncertainty quantification methods rely on approximate Bayesian inference.
The BNN family, including MC-Dropout and Stochastic Variational Inference (SVI),
estimates posterior predictive distributions through repeated stochastic forward
passes. These approaches achieve competitive NLL but scale poorly with the
number of parameters. Calibration diagnostics such as the Expected Calibration
Error (ECE) and the Brier score quantify the gap between predicted confidence
and empirical accuracy. EDL was proposed to recover calibration without the
sampling overhead that dominates BNN training budgets.

3 Methodology

3.1 Evidential Regression Formulation

Given a dataset of input-output pairs, the model emits the parameters of a
Higher-order distribution over the likelihood. For regression, the evidential
prior is a Normal-Inverse-Gamma distribution whose four parameters are predicted
by the network. The negative log-likelihood loss is augmented with an evidence
penalty term that discourages overconfidence on out-of-distribution inputs.

The evidential loss combines a likelihood term with a regularizer:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}_{NLL}(\theta) + \lambda \, \mathcal{L}_{R}(\theta)
$$

where the regularization coefficient lambda controls the strength of the
evidence penalty. Increasing lambda monotonically widens prediction intervals
and therefore reduces the risk of overconfident, poorly calibrated outputs. The
MLP backbone predicts the four evidential parameters through a shared trunk and
four regression heads.

3.2 Backbone Variants

The evidential head is mounted on three backbones. The MLP baseline consists of
four fully-connected layers. The convolutional variant extends this trunk with
spatial feature extraction. The GNN variant aggregates neighbor features over a
fixed graph structure before emitting evidential parameters. All backbones
share the identical evidential loss so that differences in uncertainty are
attributable to representation rather than to optimization.

4 Experiments

4.1 Experimental Setup

Each backbone is trained for five hundred epochs with the Adam optimizer at a
learning rate of one times ten to the minus three. The regularization
coefficient lambda is selected over a logarithmic grid from ten to the minus
three to ten to the zero. Early stopping monitors validation NLL with patience
of twenty epochs. Every configuration is repeated over five random seeds and
the mean and standard deviation are reported.

4.2 Results

The table below reports RMSE, NLL, PICP, and MPIW across backbones. Lower is
better for RMSE and NLL. PICP should approach the nominal coverage of zero point
nine, and MPIW quantifies the average interval width.

| Backbone | RMSE   | NLL   | PICP | MPIW |
|----------|--------|-------|------|------|
| MLP      | 0.412  | 1.203 | 0.91 | 1.85 |
| BNN      | 0.405  | 1.180 | 0.89 | 1.78 |
| GNN      | 0.389  | 1.140 | 0.90 | 1.69 |

The GNN backbone achieves the lowest RMSE and NLL while maintaining PICP near
the nominal level. EDL matches the BNN on NLL at a fraction of the inference
cost, since no stochastic forward passes are required at test time. The MPIW
values indicate that the GNN produces the tightest calibrated intervals,
confirming that the evidential parameters estimated over graph-structured
features are better aligned with the data.

5 Conclusion

Evidential Deep Learning separates aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty without
Monte Carlo sampling. Mounted on a GNN backbone, EDL attains the lowest RMSE and
NLL on the regression benchmark while preserving interval coverage. The
reduction in MPIW demonstrates that calibrated, tight prediction intervals are
achievable with a single deterministic forward pass. Future work will extend
the evidential formulation to classification and structured prediction tasks
where uncertainty calibration directly affects downstream decision making.
